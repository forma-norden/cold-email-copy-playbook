# copy-btl-practitioner-messaging

Use this skill to write cold emails targeting Managers, Individual Contributors,
and other below-the-line practitioners who use or evaluate tools day-to-day.

## Required Inputs

- target title and functional area
- their daily workflow context
- signal or trigger that prompted outreach
- proof element comparable to their role
- CTA type from the CTA skill

## Practitioner Messaging Principles

1. Lead with workflow pain, not strategic vision.
2. Use peer-level language. Practitioners detect vendor-speak immediately.
3. Reference the tools and processes they use daily.
4. Frame proof as time saved, friction removed, or errors eliminated.
5. Speak to their day, not their quarter.
6. Be specific about the problem. Vague pain statements get ignored.

## Structure Rules

Message blocks for BTL:

1. Opener (5 to 15 words): reference a specific workflow or tool they use
2. Pain framing (15 to 25 words): describe the friction in their actual process
3. Proof (10 to 20 words): one comparable outcome at their role level
4. CTA (5 to 15 words): tool-oriented or resource-oriented, never meeting-first

Hard constraints:

- target total: 50 to 90 words
- name a specific tool, process, or workflow they would recognize
- no C-suite language (revenue, margins, stakeholders)
- no "just checking in" or other filler lines
- CTA should offer something useful, not request time

## Execution Sequence

1. Identify the practitioner's daily workflow from their title and tools.
2. Select one signal relevant to their function (new tool adoption, team growth, process change).
3. Frame the pain as friction they experience personally, not a business outcome.
4. Attach one proof element from a peer (similar title, similar team size, similar tool stack).
5. Choose a resource-first CTA: guide, template, benchmark comparison, teardown.
6. Validate language is peer-level, not vendor-level.

## Output Contract

Return:

- 3 BTL email variants
- workflow assumption for each variant
- risk notes (tool relevance, language tone, CTA friction)
- chosen primary variant with rationale
- alternative CTA if primary CTA assumes too much knowledge

## Anti-Patterns

- using executive language for a practitioner audience
- leading with ROI instead of workflow improvement
- vague pain statements without naming a process or tool
- asking for a meeting as the first CTA
- proof from an executive peer instead of a title peer
