# copy-atl-executive-messaging

Use this skill to write cold emails targeting VP, C-Level, Director, and other
above-the-line decision-makers.

## Required Inputs

- target title and seniority level
- company context (size, stage, industry)
- signal or trigger that prompted outreach
- proof element comparable to their scope
- CTA type from the CTA skill

## Executive Messaging Principles

1. Lead with strategic impact, not features.
2. Use shorter emails (40-70 words). Executives scan, not read.
3. Reference board-level or P&L concerns, not workflow problems.
4. Frame proof as outcomes at their elevation (revenue, margin, speed-to-market).
5. Never open with self-introduction or company description.
6. One idea per email. Zero filler.

## Structure Rules

Message blocks for ATL:

1. Opener (5 to 12 words): reference a strategic signal they would notice
2. Pain framing (10 to 20 words): connect to a P&L or board-level concern
3. Proof (10 to 15 words): one comparable outcome at their scope
4. CTA (5 to 10 words): time-specific and low-commitment

Hard constraints:

- target total: 40 to 70 words
- no buzzwords or jargon the executive would filter out
- no multi-paragraph blocks
- no "I" as the first word of any sentence
- never reference your company before their problem

## Execution Sequence

1. Identify the executive's strategic scope from their title and company stage.
2. Select one signal relevant to their elevation (funding, board change, market move, hiring executive).
3. Frame the pain as a decision they own, not a task someone below them runs.
4. Attach one proof element at peer scope (similar-size company, similar-stage outcome).
5. Choose a CTA at the lowest friction level for their position (most often: "worth a look?" or route to a direct report).
6. Validate total word count is under 70.

## Output Contract

Return:

- 3 ATL email variants
- strategic framing used for each
- risk notes (relevance, proof scope match, tone calibration)
- chosen primary variant with rationale
- suggested delegation CTA if primary CTA is too direct

## Anti-Patterns

- opening with "I help companies like yours"
- using feature language instead of outcome language
- proof from a significantly smaller company than the target
- multiple asks or links
- formal language that reads like a vendor pitch
